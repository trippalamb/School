## Session Flow

The rhythm is: **activity → read textbook chunk → verbal check → exercise/activity**, cycling through. Each phase is described below in order.

Estimated total time: 25–45 minutes depending on engagement. The session has three natural stopping points (marked ✂️ below) if you need to cut early. Don't push past his engagement window.

------

## Phase 1: Opening Question and Measuring Activity

### Opening Question

Hand Link a piece of paper. Ask:

> **"How big is this paper?"**

**What you're looking for:** The question is deliberately vague. "Big" could mean long, wide, heavy, thick, area — anything. The goal is for him to bump into the ambiguity before anyone picks up a ruler.

**If he grabs a ruler and measures one side right away:** That's fine — he's engaged. After he gives you a number, ask: *"Is that how big it is?"* or *"Is that everything about how big it is?"* You're gently surfacing that "big" isn't a single measurement. Don't belabor this — just plant the seed and move on.

**If he freezes or says "I don't know":** Narrow it: *"What's one thing we could measure about it?"* or *"If you had to start somewhere, where would you start?"* The bar is "any engagement at all."

**If he gives a non-measurement answer** (like "it's regular sized" or "normal"): That's actually interesting — ask *"How would you prove that to someone?"* or *"What if I said it was small — how would you show me I'm wrong?"* This leads naturally to needing a ruler.

**The point:** Before we measure, we have to decide WHAT we're measuring. Don't teach this as a lesson — just let the moment happen and move on. It pays off later when he encounters perimeter vs. area.

------

### Main Measuring Activity

> **"Let's go measure a bunch of stuff."**

Get the rulers and tape measures. Move around the house/learning space together. Measure:

- Door frames (height and width)
- Furniture (length of a table, height of a chair)
- Small objects (a pencil, a book, a phone)
- Something organic (a banana, a leaf, his hand)
- A room or hallway (this is where the tape measure is useful)

**Guidance for you:**

- Let him handle the tools. If he's lining up the ruler wrong (not starting at zero, reading the wrong mark), gently correct in the moment — show him once, then let him try.
- Measure things in different directions (long way, short way, height, depth) but **don't point out** that you're doing this. Don't say "now let's measure in a different dimension." Just do it. If HE notices that things have more than one direction to measure, that's gold — acknowledge it and move on.
- Keep it moving. Each object should take a minute or two max. The goal is volume of experience with the tool, not precision.
- If he's enjoying himself, keep going. If he's getting bored, move to the next phase. Five or six objects is probably enough.
- Let him pick some things to measure. *"What else should we measure?"*
- **When he gives you a number, ask "X what?"** — if he says "the table is 36," ask *"36 what?"* Don't explain why yet. Just get him in the habit of including the unit. This sets up Section 1.1 (measurement = number + unit) so the textbook is confirming something he already practiced, rather than introducing it cold.

**What to watch for:**

- Can he read the ruler? Does he understand the marks between inches? (If not, that's okay — note it as a gap but don't drill it now. For today, whole numbers or "about 6 and a half inches" is fine.)
- Does he understand that you line up the zero end? (Common mistake: starting from the end of the ruler, which isn't always zero.)
- Is he saying the unit along with the number, or just the number? (Track this for your log — it tells you how much work Section 1.1 needs to do.)
- Is he making predictions? *"How long do you think this is?"* before measuring can add a challenge/game element if he seems engaged enough for it.

## Phase 2: Textbook Reading — Section 0 ("What can we count?")

After the measuring activity, sit down together. Set up the reading with:

> *"We just measured a bunch of stuff. Let's read about what we were actually doing."*

Have Link read Section 0. It's short — two paragraphs plus a vocabulary word. It introduces:

- You can count all sorts of things, including time and space
- **Units of measurement** — the thing you're counting when you measure (seconds for time, inches/centimeters for distance)
- Rulers and number lines both count distance

### Verbal Check

After he reads it, ask conversationally:

- *"When we measured the table, what were we counting?"* (inches, centimeters — whatever unit they used)
- *"What's the vocabulary word for that?"* (unit of measurement)
- *"Can you think of a unit we use to measure time?"* (seconds, minutes, hours — any of these)

**If he gets into the number line connection:** The textbook says counting on a number line is also counting distance. If this clicks for him, that's great — let him sit with it for a moment. If it goes nowhere, don't push. It comes back later.

**If he seems restless after reading:** That's normal — he just came off a physical activity. Keep the check brief (1–2 questions) and move to the next reading chunk quickly. The activity bridge below is very short on purpose.

### Activity Bridge (30 seconds)

Point at the ruler (or one of the objects you measured earlier) and ask:

> *"So when we measured the door frame and I asked '36 what?' — what's the answer in textbook language?"*

You're looking for something like "36 inches — inches is the unit of measurement." If he gets close, that's enough. Move on.

## Phase 3: Textbook Reading — Section 1 Intro ("How long is a line?")

> *"Okay, everything we measured today had something in common. The next part of the book is about that."*

Have Link read the Section 1 introduction. It's about three paragraphs. It introduces:

- **Line** — infinitely long; goes on forever in both directions; can't be measured
- **Line segment** — the part of a line between two **points**; this is what you actually measure
- **Point** — an exact location in space
- In everyday life, people say "line" when they mean "line segment"

### Verbal Check

- *"So what's the difference between a line and a line segment?"*
- *"Can you measure a line? Why not?"*
- *"When you measured the door frame — was that a line or a line segment?"*

**If he's into the infinity angle:** Let him explore it. *"How long is a line?"* → infinitely long. *"What if you added more to it?"* → still infinitely long. This is genuine math and he likes infinity — don't rush past it. But also don't let it consume the session. A couple minutes of "whoa, infinity" is great, then gently steer back.

**If he's not into it or seems confused:** Don't force the infinity hook. The key takeaway is simpler: everything he measured today had a start and an end, and that's called a line segment. That's enough.

**If comprehension seems shaky:** Read through it together a second time. Point at the pictures. *"See, this one has endpoints — segment. This one has arrows — it keeps going."*

### Activity Bridge (30 seconds)

> *"Can you point at a line segment in this room?"*

Anything with a visible start and end works — the edge of the table, the door frame, a pencil. If he points at something, he's got it. Move on.

## Phase 4: Textbook Reading — Section 1.1 ("How can we measure distance?")

> *"You already know how to use a ruler. This part is about making sure we agree on how it works — and one rule you've already been following without knowing it."*

Have Link read Section 1.1. About three paragraphs. It covers:

- A ruler is a line segment with evenly spaced marks
- Line up the zero mark (watch out — zero isn't always at the edge)
- **Measurement = a number + a unit.** "5" isn't a measurement. "5 inches" is.

### Verbal Check

- *"What two things do you need for a measurement?"* (a number and a unit)
- *"If someone says the table is 5 — what's wrong with that?"* (5 what?)
- *"Remember when I kept asking you '36 what?' earlier? That's what this is about."*

The last question is the payoff — connect the reading to what they did in the measuring activity. The textbook is naming something he already practiced, not introducing something new.

**If the "zero isn't always at the edge" part confuses him:** Grab a ruler and show him. Point at where the zero is. Show him one where the zero IS at the edge if you have one, and one where it's inset. This is concrete — let him look at the actual object.

------

## Phase 5: Exercise — Measuring Drawn Line Segments

**Setup:** Before the session (or during a quick break), draw 5–6 line segments on a blank piece of paper. Make them different lengths — some short (1–2 inches), some longer (5–6 inches), some at slight angles. Don't label them.

**Instructions to Link:**

> *"I drew some line segments. Can you measure each one and write down the measurement?"*

**Important change from the old version:** Because Section 1.1 now explicitly teaches measurement = number + unit, check that he's writing both the number AND the unit. If he writes just "4" — ask *"Is that a measurement?"* and see if he self-corrects. If not, prompt: *"What's missing?"*

He does this independently while you observe.

**What you're checking:**

- Can he use the ruler accurately on his own?
- Is he reading the measurements correctly?
- Is he recording the number AND the unit?

**If he's struggling with the ruler:** Sit beside him and work through one together. Show the zero alignment, how to read the nearest mark. Then have him try the next one on his own.

**If he's writing just numbers without units:** This is the most likely gap. Don't make it a big deal. Just point to one and ask *"36 what?"* — callback to earlier.

**If he breezes through it:** Great — move on.

------

## Phase 6: Activity — Role Reversal

> *"Now it's your turn. Draw some line segments, measure them, and then see if I can get them right."*

**Rules:**

- He draws line segments on paper.
- He measures each one and writes down the full measurement (number + unit) — this is important. He has to know the answer to check yours.
- He gives the paper to you (Abby) and you measure them.

**Your role:**

- Get most of them right, but miss one or two by a little. *"Hmm, I got 4 inches — is that right?"*
- **Try "forgetting" the unit once.** Write down just "3" and see if he catches it. If he does — that's the Section 1.1 concept clicking. If he doesn't, no big deal; it'll keep coming up.
- Let him correct you. This puts him in the authority position.
- If he caught your mistakes: *"Good catch. How far off was I?"*

**What this does:**

- Reinforces his measurement skills (he has to measure accurately to check you)
- Flips the power dynamic — he's the expert, you're the student
- Bypasses "I can't" because the frame is about testing YOU, not him
- The unit-forgetting move reinforces measurement = number + unit without teaching it again

------

## Phase 7: Wrap-Up

Keep this short. Ask:

> *"What did you figure out today?"*

Let him answer in his own words. If he's stuck, prompt lightly: *"What did we use? What did we call the things we measured?"*

Then review the vocabulary together — not as a memorization task, just a quick look: *"These are the words for the stuff you just did."*

**Vocabulary to confirm he's seen (across Section 0, Section 1, and Section 1.1):**

- Unit of measurement (Section 0)
- Line (Section 1)
- Line segment (Section 1)
- Point (Section 1)
- Measurement — a number and a unit together (Section 1.1)

## ⚠️ ATTENTION: What's Different from the Old Guide

These are the substantive changes from the original teaching guide, not just reordering. Flagging them so nothing gets lost.

1. **"X what?" prompt during measuring activity (Phase 1).** New. The old guide didn't have Abby prompting for units during the hands-on activity because the textbook didn't teach measurement = number + unit until later. Now that Section 1.1 explicitly covers this, the measuring activity should seed it. This is a light touch — she's just asking the question, not explaining why.
2. **Three reading chunks instead of one.** The old session had Link read one block about line vs. line segment. Now there are three short blocks (Section 0, Section 1 intro, Section 1.1), each followed by a verbal check and a brief activity bridge. The total reading volume is higher. The early stopping points are there in case it's too much.
3. **Exercise now checks for units.** The drawn-line-segment exercise is the same, but the success criteria changed. Before, accurate measurement was enough. Now, writing the full measurement (number + unit) is part of the check.
4. **Role reversal has a unit-forgetting move.** Small addition — Abby deliberately "forgets" the unit once to see if Link catches it.
5. **Programming bonus removed from this session.** It's now positioned after Section 1.2 in the textbook, so it moves to Session 2. The computer doesn't need to be set up for Session 1.
6. **No tape measure / ruler distinction is taught.** The textbook doesn't distinguish between these tools — it just talks about rulers. But the measuring activity uses both. This is fine (experience before formalization), just noting it so you're aware the textbook won't explicitly name the tape measure.

## ⚠️ ATTENTION: Possible Issues to Watch

1. **Section 0's fruit analogy.** The textbook says "seconds are a type of something called units; just like apples are a type of fruit." This is an analogy for category membership — seconds belong to the category "units" like apples belong to "fruit." If Link reads this literally or gets confused by the comparison, Abby can simplify: *"It's just saying that 'inches' is a kind of unit, the same way 'apple' is a kind of fruit. It's a type of thing."*
2. **Reading load risk.** Three reading blocks is a lot for a kid who alternates frequently. The activity bridges are designed to break it up, but if you see glazing over during the second or third read, just stop. The early stopping points are real — use them.
3. **"Measurement" is doing double duty.** In Section 1.1, "measurement" is formally defined as number + unit together. But in everyday language (and earlier in the session), they've been "measuring things" without that formal definition. This shouldn't be confusing — the textbook handles it naturally — but if Link asks "wait, haven't we been measuring this whole time?" the answer is yes, and now we have a name for the thing you write down.
