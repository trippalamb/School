## Section 0: "What can we count?"

before you have him read try to get him to figure it all out first. by asking him:

- what kind of things can he count?
- lead him into more abstract things to count like steps and heartbeats
- if he gets to seconds or distance awesome! if not try to lead him to distance by suggesting seconds

Once he understands he can count distance have him read the next section. Hopefully this can help him feel like this won't be difficult and it sets precedent that he can figure out the answers to some of the questions on his own.

There is one vocabulary question so you guys can start practicing learning the definitions of words and repeating them. Have him write the definition by copying it by hand.

## Section 1: "How long is a line?"

#### Reading comprehension: 

Have him read the section by himself and then ask him some questions about it:

- how long is a line?
- can you draw a line? can you draw a line segment?
- can you draw a point?
- When people talk in everyday life what do they usually mean when they say line?

If he doesn't get the questions have him read it again with the questions in mind or read it with him depending on his vibe.

### Section 1.1: "How can we measure distance?"

#### Activity

You'll need to gather

- At least one ruler (with inches on one side, centimeters on the other is ideal)
- A retractable tape measure
- A floppy tape measure

Go measure a bunch of things. Make you you at least measure 1 of the following:

- organic shaped
- rigid
- floppy
- body part
- room
- book
- geometric shaped
- something really difficult to even figure out what part to measure

make sure he

- notices there may be more than one direction to measure something
- does the majority of the measuring himself
- that he lines it up correctly

#### Reading Comprehension

Get him to explain how to use a ruler

potential questions:

- where do you start measuring from with a ruler? (the zero)
- what is a measurement made of?

#### Exercises

Draw several line segments on a piece of paper at integer inch lengths (1 inch, 2 inches, 5, etc. ). Have him measure and write the length of each beside the segment

Then do a role reversal: Have him draw several line segments and have you measure them. For him to know if you got it right he'll have to measure them himself first

### Section 1.2: "Can you measure with toilet paper?"

You can do reading comprehension or the activity first here. Whichever feels better at the time

#### Reading Comprehension

For this one let him read back through the section as reference to answer the questions if needed

Questions:

- What are some types of units of measurement for distance?
- What are some sillier and less useful units of measurement? (shoes, toilet paper)
- why are those less useful?

#### Activity

For this activity you will use toilet paper to measure distance.  Measure at least one big something like a room, rug, or dining room table. If there are two rolls of toilet paper with different square lengths that is even better. 

> Use a marker to help make the perforated edges more visible if needed

If you have two kinds of toilet paper make sure to show him that they both give different measurements.

Have him pick something to invent his own unit of measurement. If he is vibing well with the activity get him to pick one small thing and one bigger thing (like inches and feet). Try to guide him away from picking his foot (its too obvious and easy).

If he has trouble picking guide him but try to make him pick something himself.



### Vocabulary

## Section 2: "How can we tell someone else how long something is?"

read the first section together. make sure he is tracking with the title question and the ones in the paragraph. see if he can answer them at all already.

### Section 2.1: What's a Label?

#### Reading Comprehension/Activity

Ask him to make a labeled diagram of something. For example he has to draw the dining room table, measure it, and then label it. Try to get him to work out the three parts to the diagram on his own without explicitly telling him what they are



### Section 2.2: Which One Is Bigger?

#### Reading Comprehension

Get him to stop before the examples. You guys can do that together after comprehension.

Questions:

- Does a bigger picture always have to be for a bigger object? If he has trouble here so him examples of paintings and books from around the house
- what does scaling mean? to scale? scaled up? scaled down?

#### Examples

Work through the examples together.

#### Exercises

Give him 1+ labeled diagram and have him give you the length of the object and the length of the drawing as separate answers

Give him 5 or so labeled line segment pairs/triplets and have him circle the one that is labeled the largest. 

Make sure:

- sometimes the label and size match and some times they don't
- make some the same length but different labels
- make some really big numbers

Bonus to make him think:

Draw a line segment with length but label it 0 and ask him if that is a valid label or not. The correct answer is that it isn't, but the important part is for him to think about it and try to work out an answer with a reason

### Section 2.3: Do We Always Need Units?

#### Reading Comprehension

Questions:

- Can you add together measurements if they have different units?
- If you add together 2 apples and 2 action figures what do you get? Is that helpful? Why or why not?
- Do you always need units?

### Vocabulary

Get him to copy the definitions by hand onto his own piece of paper.

Draw a circle and ask him to draw a to scale, scaled up, and scaled down version and label each.
